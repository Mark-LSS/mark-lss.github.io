01
What Is Ultrathermic Cutting?
Introduction to oxy-arc cutting technology — Broco system

Oxygen-arc (oxy-arc) cutting is a thermal cutting process in which metal is severed by the chemical reaction of oxygen with the base metal at elevated temperatures. An electric arc brings the metal to its kindling temperature, after which a high-velocity jet of pure oxygen directed through a tubular cutting rod sustains the exothermic reaction and ejects the molten oxide from the cut.

The Broco ultrathermic system is distinct from conventional oxy-arc in that the cutting rod itself contains exothermic core wires. Once ignited, the rod burns independently of the arc — meaning the rod will continue burning even if electrical power is removed. This is the defining characteristic of the system and drives the most critical safety rules.

☠️ CRITICAL — Rod Continues Burning Without Power
A burning Broco ultrathermic rod CANNOT be extinguished by switching off the welding generator. It will continue to burn as long as oxygen flows. The ONLY method to extinguish a burning rod is to release the oxygen lever on the cutting torch.

Two Energy Sources

DC arc for initial ignition + exothermic chemical reaction for sustained burn

Subsea Application

Primary method for commercial diver cutting of structural steel, piles, pipework, and non-ferrous materials

Rod-Based

Each cut consumes rod material. Typical 9.5 mm rod cuts 300–450 mm in 12 mm plate

High Risk

Fatal accidents have occurred. Every cut requires a written risk assessment and JSA

Key Definitions

02
How the Process Works
The oxy-arc cycle from ignition to rod change

The Broco system combines two independent energy sources. Understanding both is critical to safe operation:

1
Pre-Purge (4 seconds minimum)

Diver opens oxygen lever. Pure oxygen flows through rod bore for a minimum of 4 seconds before calling for power. This purges water and any gas pockets from the rod bore and establishes full O₂ flow.

2
Arc Ignition — "Make It Hot"

Diver calls Make it hot. Tender closes knife switch. DC arc (electrode negative) strikes at rod tip, rapidly raising base metal to kindling temperature.

3
Exothermic Reaction Begins

Base metal oxidises. The exothermic core wires in the rod ignite and the ultrathermic burn begins. The rod is now self-sustaining — the arc is no longer needed to maintain the cut.

4
Cutting and Slag Ejection

Oxygen jet ejects molten oxide (slag) from the kerf. Diver maintains rod contact with the work at 30°–90°. Power is called off with Take it off but rod continues burning.

5
Extinguishing and Rod Change

Diver releases oxygen lever — exothermic burn ceases. Rod stub (minimum 75 mm from collet nut) is removed, inspected, and replaced. Never burn rod closer than 75 mm from collet nut.

⚡ Verbal Commands — Standardised and Non-Negotiable
Diver CommandTender ActionEffect
Make it hotClose knife switchElectrical power to rod — enables arc ignition
Take it offOpen knife switchPower removed — rod continues burning on O₂
Shut it downClose oxygen valve at regulatorEmergency stop — O₂ cut, burn extinguishes

Understand the full cutting cycle before proceeding.

03
Hazards & Danger Zones
Comprehensive hazard identification — IMCA D 003 §3.3
☠️ Oxy-Arc Cutting Is a High-Risk Offshore Activity
Underwater explosions from oxy-arc cutting have occurred in industry and some have been fatal. Every cut requires a thorough, documented risk assessment prior to commencement.

Major Hazard Categories

💥

Gas / Explosion — CRITICAL

Explosive gas mixtures (O₂ + H₂) ignited by cutting spark; gas entrapment in enclosed spaces

⚡

Electrical — CRITICAL

Electrocution risk from incorrect polarity, AC use, poor earthing, or SCUBA use

🔥

Thermal — HIGH

Burns from molten slag ejection; diver suit damage; topside fire risk from O₂ enrichment

👁️

Physical / Structural — HIGH

Ejected slag; sharp cut edges; structural collapse of cut members; dropped objects

SCUBA Prohibition — Absolute Rule

🤿 SCUBA EQUIPMENT IS PROHIBITED — NO EXCEPTIONS
Two independent reasons: (1) Electrocution risk — current can travel from the workpiece through the water, up the diver's body, and into the SCUBA mouthpiece/regulator, with fatal outcome. (2) No hardwire communications — verbal commands cannot be confirmed, creating a dual-control failure. IMCA D 003 §3.2 mandates surface supply diving for all oxy-arc cutting operations.

Danger Zone — Diver's Position

The diver must always position himself so that:

  • Slag and sparks are directed away from the umbilical, suit, and body
  • The diver is not downstream of the oxygen jet (no hyperoxic exposure)
  • The diver has a clear escape route — never cut yourself into a confined space
  • The diver is never directly under a falling or collapsing cut member

Read all hazard categories before proceeding.

04
Gas Explosion Risks
Primary fatal hazard in oxy-arc cutting — enclosed members
💥 Explosion Risk Increases with Depth
Oxygen pressure at depth is higher, and any trapped gas expands to a greater absolute pressure. An explosion at 30m is significantly more powerful than the same gas volume at surface.

Mechanism of Explosion

An explosion occurs when all three of the following conditions exist simultaneously:

① Explosive Gas Present

O₂ + H₂ mixture (electrolysis product), entrapped methane, or O₂-enriched atmosphere inside a member

② Enclosed Space

Pile, caisson, void, pipe section, sealed jacket member, structural node, or any space with no vent path

③ Ignition Source

Cutting rod tip breaches the member wall while gas is present — arc or exothermic heat is sufficient

Mandatory Controls — Enclosed Members

1
Vent Hole — Mandatory First Action

Before commencing the main cut on any enclosed structural member (pile, caisson, void, node), cut a vent hole above and behind the planned cut position. Confirm the vent hole is open and venting before proceeding.

2
Verify Vent Path

The vent hole must be positioned so that any trapped gas escapes upward and away from the diver and heat source. Confirm the vent is unobstructed before main cut.

3
Do Not Re-Seal the Vent

Never allow the vent to be sealed by slag or debris during the main cut. If the vent blocks, stop cutting, clear the vent, and re-confirm before resuming.

⚠️ Never Assume a Member Is Empty
Corroded or fouled drain holes may be blocked. Hydrogen is produced by cathodic protection and electrolysis. Any enclosed member must be treated as potentially containing explosive gas until venting confirms otherwise.

Understand the vent hole requirement before proceeding.

05
Electrical Hazards
Polarity, grounding, shock prevention and circuit control
⚡ AC Power Will Kill the Diver
Using alternating current (AC) for underwater cutting will electrocute the diver. NEVER use AC power. Use ONLY DC from a known, verified power source. Polarity must be checked and confirmed before any diver enters the water.

Correct Polarity — Critical

Cutting Torch / Rod

➖

Electrode NEGATIVE (−). Connected to negative terminal of DC generator

Ground Clamp (G-Clamp)

➕

Ground clamp POSITIVE (+). Connected to positive terminal of DC generator

Interactive: Polarity Check

In the salt water polarity test, which connection produces a heavy stream of bubbles when correct polarity (DC electrode negative) is confirmed?

Cutting Rod Tip
Heavy bubbles from rod

Ground Clamp Placement

📍 G-Clamp Position Rule
The earth/ground G-clamp must be connected to redundant steelwork, cleaned to bare metal, not positioned such that the diver is in the electrical circuit between the torch and the clamp. The clamp must never be placed on the vessel hull or on the diver's umbilical.

Knife Switch — Default Position

The knife switch controlling electrical supply to the cutting torch must be in the OPEN (isolated) position at all times. It is closed only when the diver specifically calls Make it hot. It is opened immediately on Take it off.

⚠️ Broco Rods Only — No Substitution
Using underwater cutting rods from other manufacturers can cause flashback and torch damage. Only Broco rods are approved for use in the Broco BR-22 Plus torch.
🚫 Burning Bar — Prohibited
A "burning bar" (diameter >9.5 mm OR length >914 mm) is prohibited under all Broco and IMCA guidelines. Using oversized rods generates heat and oxygen flows beyond the torch design parameters and may destroy the torch.

Rod Storage

  • Store rods in manufacturer's sealed tube — never expose to seawater or moisture before use
  • Inspect rod tips before use — bent, corroded, or contaminated tips must not be used
  • Rods removed from the water and not used must be inspected and dried before re-use

09
Pre-Use Inspection
Mandatory checks before every deployment — no shortcuts

O-Ring and Seal Inspection

Before any operation, all O-rings and seals must be inspected. Use oxygen-safe silicone grease on all O-rings. Inspect for cuts, flattening, or extrusion. Replace any degraded O-rings before use.

Pressure Test — 9.0 bar[g]

📋 Mandatory 9.0 bar[g] Leak Test
The assembled torch must be pressure tested at 9.0 bar[g] with oxygen before every deployment. Submerge in a bucket of clean water and check all connections for bubbles. Zero leakage is the acceptance criterion. Any leak = reject and repair before deployment.

Pre-Use Checklist

✓
Torch body

Inspect insulation for cracks, burns or damage. Check cable lug secure.

✓
Collet and collet nut

Inspect collet washer — do not lubricate. Check collet nut thread condition.

✓
Flash arrestor

In-date certification. No physical damage. Replace as complete unit only.

✓
O₂ hose

No cuts, kinks, or abrasion. Fittings secure and thread-sealed with oxygen-safe green Teflon tape.

✓
Oxygen lever

Opens and closes freely. Positive snap to closed position.

✓
Pressure test

Assembled system to 9.0 bar[g] oxygen. Zero leakage confirmed and recorded.

10
Assembly & Setup
Surface preparation — assembling the complete system before deployment
⚠️ Complete Steps in Sequence
Do not proceed to the next step until the current step is confirmed complete. All components must be clean, oil-free, and inspected.
1
O₂ Supply — Regulator and Flash Arrestor

Connect regulator to O₂ cylinder. Attach flash arrestor to regulator outlet. Confirm flash arrestor is in-date and serviceable.

2
Hose Connection

Connect O₂ hose to flash arrestor. Use oxygen-safe green Teflon tape. Ensure no petroleum-based sealants used anywhere in O₂ circuit.

3
Torch Connection

Connect torch to hose. Check insulation body, collet assembly, and O-rings. Pressure test assembled torch to 9.0 bar[g].

4
Polarity Check

Confirm DC generator. Check and confirm electrode negative (−), ground clamp positive (+). Perform salt water polarity test if any doubt.

5
Rod Loading

Insert rod recessed end first into collet. Tighten collet nut finger-tight then ¼ turn with wrench. Confirm minimum 75 mm protrudes past collet nut.

6
Electrical Isolation Confirmation

Confirm knife switch in OPEN position before diver enters water. System ready for deployment.

✓ Reference
IMCA D 003 Appendix 2 — Surface Preparation Checklist. This checklist must be completed and signed before every deployment.

Settings & Pressure Calculator
Oxygen regulator pressure and amperage tables with live calculator

Oxygen Pressure Formula

Gauge pressure [bar(g)] = 6.21 + (0.101 × depth[m]) + (0.0226 × hose length[m])

The base value of 6.21 bar[g] is the minimum required at the rod tip regardless of depth. Additional pressure compensates for hydrostatic head (depth) and friction losses (hose length).

12
Deployment Procedure
Full JSA-compliant deployment sequence — IMCA D 003 §4.5.1

Pre-Deployment Requirements

  • JSA completed and relayed to all relevant parties
  • Client approval and Permit to Work (PTW) confirmed in place
  • Dive Supervisor has reviewed the project risk assessment
  • Fire watcher designated topside
  • Deck area clear of hydrocarbons; cordoned off
  • Equipment function tested on deck

Subsea Site Assessment

1
Identify and Independently Validate Cut Location

Diver identifies and marks location. A second diver or ROV validates independently before any cutting. Wrong-location cuts have occurred.

2
Survey for Hazards

Inspect for live cables, hydraulic lines, risers, hydrocarbons, impressed current anodes, and proximity to other structures. Document on JSA.

3
Assess Enclosed Members

Identify any enclosed structural members in or adjacent to the cut path. Plan vent holes before main cut (Section 04).

4
Earth Clamp Positioning

G-clamp positioned on clean bare metal, redundant steelwork. Position confirmed as outside the diver's electrical circuit.

📋 IMCA D 003 §7 — Right to Refuse
Any personnel involved in an oxy-arc cutting task who is not personally confident in their competence to perform the task safely has the explicit right AND responsibility to refuse. This right is non-negotiable and cannot be overridden by client, supervisor, or commercial pressure.

Understand the pre-deployment requirements and right to refuse.

13
Cutting Operations
Step-by-step subsea cutting procedure — rod insertion to completion

Diver Position and Body Control

  • Stable two-handed control of torch at all times
  • Rod angle: 30° to 90° to the work surface depending on material thickness
  • Umbilical kept clear of slag ejection path at all times
  • Diver not positioned where falling cut material or slag can contact suit or face

Cutting Sequence

1
Rod Insertion and Confirmation

Rod inserted recessed end first. Collet nut tightened. Minimum 75 mm past collet face confirmed.

2
Oxygen Pre-Purge — 4 Seconds

Diver opens oxygen lever and holds for minimum 4 seconds. This purges water from rod bore and establishes full O₂ flow. Do not call for power before 4 seconds minimum.

3
"Make It Hot" — Request Power

Diver calls Make it hot. Tender closes knife switch. Arc ignites rod at workpiece. Ultrathermic burn commences.

4
Cutting

Advance rod steadily. Maintain contact. Do not lift the rod from the work. Work at controlled pace. Half gas volume maximum for working diver (per gas management plan).

5
"Take It Off" and Rod Change

Call Take it off when rod is consumed to 75 mm mark. Release oxygen lever — burn extinguishes. Remove stub. Load new rod. Repeat from Step 2.

6
End of Cut — "Shut It Down"

On completion, call Shut it down. Tender closes O₂ valve at regulator. Both knife switch and O₂ valve isolated. Diver confirms rod extinguished before moving torch.

The 4-second pre-purge and command words are exam questions.

14
Techniques by Material
Material-specific methods, rod selection and special procedures

Click each material type to expand cutting guidance.

Use 6 mm rod for steel up to 12 mm thick for maximum economy and a narrow kerf. Continuous power recommended — maintaining ignition is harder without it at this diameter. Achieves 500–635 mm cut in 6 mm plate; 250–380 mm in 12 mm plate. Slower advance for thicker sections.
Use 9.5 mm rod for steel 12 mm and thicker. Better production in bad visibility or thick sections. Achieves 300–450 mm in 12 mm plate. Faster slag ejection requires more attention to diver positioning relative to ejection path. Standard choice for pile cutting.
Use 9.5 mm rod. Higher amperage (300–400 A) and oxygen pressure required. Stainless oxidises more slowly — work more slowly and keep rod in contact. Cast iron is brittle — avoid applying force to the cut section during cutting.
Use 9.5 mm rod only. For sections over 75 mm thick, increase O₂ pressure to 7.58 bar[g] over ambient. Non-ferrous metals do not oxidise in the same way — the rod melts the material rather than oxidising it. Work slowly and maintain contact. Higher amperage required.
9.5 mm rod is mandatory — no alternative permitted. These non-conductive materials require the additional exothermic energy of the larger rod. The 6 mm rod does not provide sufficient energy to cut non-conductive materials effectively or safely. Concrete produces considerable debris — diver positioning is critical.
Short bursts of oxygen without arc are sufficient for synthetic rope and wood. Use minimum O₂ flow. Contact ignition method. Do not sustain cutting for extended periods — fire risk on wooden structures is high if O₂ enrichment occurs in gaps or voids.
Use multiple passes. First pass opens a groove to the required depth. Subsequent passes widen the kerf until the section is severed. For plates over 100 mm, consider using a piercing technique — rotate the rod in the initial contact point to drill through before advancing the cut.

Rod selection by material is an exam question.

Dos & Don'ts
Critical safety rules organised by hazard category

Oxygen and Equipment

✓ Do

  • Use oxygen-safe silicone grease on all O-rings
  • Use oxygen-safe green Teflon tape on thread fittings
  • Maintain O₂ purity at minimum 99.5%
  • Store torch in a dry, clean, oil-free environment
  • Replace flash arrestor assembly as a complete unit
  • Pressure test at 9.0 bar[g] before every deployment
  • Use Broco rods exclusively with the Broco BR-22 torch

✗ Don't

  • Never use oil or petroleum-based grease on any torch part
  • Never use standard white Teflon tape — O₂-safe green only
  • Never test O₂ hose with compressed air
  • Never use rods from other manufacturers
  • Never use a "burning bar" — dia >9.5 mm or >914 mm length
  • Never disassemble the flash arrestor — replace complete
  • Never lubricate the collet washer

Electrical

✓ Do

  • Use DC only — electrode negative (−)
  • Perform polarity test before diver enters water
  • Keep knife switch OPEN except when "Make it hot" is called
  • Position G-clamp outside diver's electrical circuit
  • Confirm hardwire communications before deployment

✗ Don't

  • Never use AC power — it will kill the diver
  • Never allow SCUBA equipment for oxy-arc cutting
  • Never energise the rod until diver calls "Make it hot"
  • Never allow energised rod to be lifted clear of the work
  • Never place G-clamp on vessel hull or umbilical

Cutting Operations

✓ Do

  • Pre-purge oxygen for minimum 4 seconds before calling power
  • Cut vent holes in all enclosed members before main cut
  • Stop cutting before rod burns within 75 mm of collet nut
  • Keep umbilical clear of slag ejection path at all times
  • Use standardised verbal commands only

✗ Don't

  • Never cut an enclosed member without venting first
  • Never burn a rod closer than 75 mm from collet nut
  • Never lift a burning rod away from the work and wave it
  • Never attempt to stop the burn by lifting rod from water
  • Never start cutting if not personally confident — refuse the task

17
Troubleshooting
Diagnosing and resolving common operational problems
ℹ️ Most Problems Stem from Incorrect Technique
Broco ultrathermic rods require higher O₂ flow, lower amperage, and direct contact with the work — not maintenance of an arc gap. Operators familiar with conventional steel tubular rods frequently apply wrong setup to ultrathermic rods.
Symptoms: Rod burns longer than 60 seconds; cuts less than 300 mm in 12 mm plate; oxygen jet barely visible.
Cause: Regulator pressure too low; kinked hose; partially closed valve; blocked flash arrestor; incorrect depth calculation.
Action: Recalculate required pressure using formula. Check hose and valves. Replace flash arrestor if blocked.
Symptoms: Arc strikes but no sustained burn begins.
Cause: Incorrect polarity (most common); insufficient amperage; rod not in contact with work; wet or corroded rod tip; O₂ flow too low to sustain burn.
Action: Check polarity first. Confirm amperage settings. Touch rod firmly to work. Increase O₂ pressure if correct polarity confirmed.
Symptoms: Loud pop or bang felt at torch; torch may be hot or damaged.
Cause: Spontaneous combustion of trapped oxygen at cutting point; insufficient pre-purge; rod inserted wrong way; contamination in O₂ circuit.
Action: STOP immediately. Release O₂ lever. Recover to surface. Inspect torch and flash arrestor. Replace flash arrestor as complete unit before re-deploying. Report incident.
Symptoms: "Make it hot" called — no arc response at rod tip.
Cause: Knife switch open or faulty; cable connection loose at torch lug or generator; G-clamp poor contact; wrong polarity (electrode positive — no arc).
Action: Check knife switch position. Check all cable connections. Confirm polarity. Check G-clamp contact.
Symptoms: Rod consumes very slowly; cutting is very slow; rod gets hot at collet end.
Cause: Amperage too high; not maintaining rod contact with work; using wrong rod for material; lifting rod away from work to maintain "arc gap".
Action: Reduce amperage. Maintain firm rod contact — do not try to maintain an arc gap. Confirm correct rod selection for material.

Know the common causes and corrective actions.

Maintenance & Certification
Post-use procedures, cleaning, storage and IMCA D 018 certification

Immediate Post-Use Procedures

1
Disconnect and Cap O₂ Hose

Disconnect cutting torch from umbilical. Install temporary cap on oxygen hose fitting immediately to prevent contamination and water ingress.

2
Fresh Water Wash and Dry

Wash cutting torch thoroughly with fresh water. Remove all salt deposits and slag. Dry completely — do not store wet.

3
O-Ring Inspection and Re-Lubrication

Remove collet assembly. Inspect all O-rings. Apply oxygen-safe silicone grease to all O-rings (except collet washer). Replace any O-rings showing cuts, flattening, or damage.

4
Component Inspection

Inspect insulation body, cable lug, collet nut, and collet for damage. Inspect flash arrestor. Log any deficiencies. Remove serviceable components from any torch requiring workshop repair.

5
Storage

Store torch in dry, clean, oil-free environment. Cap all open connections. Store rods in manufacturer's sealed tubes away from moisture.
