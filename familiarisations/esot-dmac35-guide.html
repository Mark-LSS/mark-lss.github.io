2. Background Concepts (Sections 01–03 of the Guide)

2.1 Section 01 — Why Oxygen Becomes Toxic

This section covers the physiological foundation: why breathing oxygen under pressure, for long enough, damages lung tissue. Two types of oxygen toxicity are introduced:

The key insight from Section 01 is that pulmonary oxygen toxicity (POT) is dose-dependent and largely reversible. By tracking ESOT and observing mandatory rest periods, divers can work safely across consecutive days.

What Is Oxygen Toxicity?

Oxygen keeps us alive — but under pressure, breathing too much of it over time damages your lungs. This is the fundamental problem ESOT and DMAC 35 exist to manage.

Oxygen Under Pressure

At depth, pressure increases. Every 10 metres of seawater adds 1 atmosphere (atm) of pressure. This means the partial pressure of oxygen (pO₂) in your breathing gas increases even if the percentage of O₂ in the mix stays the same.

Divers breathing nitrox (oxygen-enriched air) or pure oxygen during decompression face significantly elevated pO₂ — which is what causes oxygen toxicity over time.

🔢
Simple Pressure Rule

pO₂ (atm) = Absolute pressure (atm) × Fraction of O₂
At 30m breathing Nitrox 36%: pO₂ = (30/10 + 1) × 0.36 = 4.0 × 0.36 = 1.44 atm
You will learn to calculate this precisely in Section 03 — the pO₂ Calculator.

Two Types of Oxygen Toxicity

⚡
CNS Toxicity — Acute (managed separately)

At very high pO₂ (typically >1.6 atm), oxygen can trigger sudden convulsions — dangerous underwater. Managed by separate CNS clock limits. ESOT and DMAC 35 do not cover CNS toxicity.

🫁
Pulmonary Oxygen Toxicity (POT) — Cumulative

Sustained hyperoxic exposure damages lung tissue progressively. Symptoms: cough, chest tightness, shortness of breath — measured as reduction in Vital Capacity (VC). This is exactly what ESOT measures and DMAC 35 limits.

Why Does It Matter for Working Divers?

A single dive rarely causes concern. But surface-oriented occupational divers may dive every day, sometimes multiple times per day, breathing high-pO₂ gases including nitrox bottom mixes and pure oxygen during surface decompression (SurDO₂). Without tracking cumulative oxygen exposure, lung function can decline progressively — even when each individual dive seems modest.

✓
Good News: POT Is Manageable and Largely Reversible

Pulmonary oxygen toxicity is dose-dependent. By tracking ESOT and staying within DMAC 35 limits — including mandatory overnight recovery — divers can work safely across consecutive days. The science behind these limits is exactly what this guide teaches.

2.2 Section 02 — The Science Behind ESOT

This section traces the development of oxygen exposure indices from UPTD (1970) through Arieli K to ESOT, explaining why each step was an improvement.

UPTD — The Old System

The Unit Pulmonary Toxic Dose was the standard for over 50 years. The formula (DMAC 35 Equation 1) is:

UPTD has two significant limitations for surface-oriented diving: (1) it is inaccurate for short exposures (<5 hours) above 1 atm — which is the most common scenario in commercial diving — and (2) it has no validated recovery function, so it cannot be used to calculate how long to rest between dives. The Repex exposure limits (Hamilton et al. 1988) that used UPTD were based primarily on expert opinion rather than controlled scientific data.

ESOT — The Current System

Equivalent Surface Oxygen Time was introduced by Risberg, van Ooij and Matity (2023) as a simplified, more accurate alternative. The formula (DMAC 35 Equations 2 and 3) is:

The reference point is intuitive: ESOT = 1 means one minute of breathing 100% O₂ at surface pressure (pO₂ = 1.0 atm). Every other combination scales from there. At pO₂ = 1.4 atm, kp = 1.4^2.285 = 2.16, so the same dive time generates 2.16 times more ESOT than breathing pure O₂ at the surface.

Arieli K (mentioned but not used operationally)

Arieli K is a more complex toxicity index that ESOT was designed to approximate. It predicts POT development with equal accuracy to ESOT but requires computational effort that makes it impractical in the field. ESOT is a mathematical transformation of Arieli K. You do not need to calculate Arieli K.

The Evolution of Oxygen Monitoring

Five decades of science produced progressively better ways to measure oxygen toxicity risk. Each step solved a specific problem with the approach before it.

1970 · Bardin & Lambertsen
UPTD / OTU
SUPERSEDED

Unit Pulmonary Toxic Dose — also known as OTU (Oxygen Toxicity Unit). Used as the standard for over 50 years. The classic Repex limits (Hamilton et al. 1988) set 850 UPTD as a single-day maximum, reducing to 300 UPTD/day for long-duration diving.

The problem: UPTD is not an accurate predictor of POT for short (<5h) exposures exceeding 1 atm — precisely the most common scenario in surface-oriented diving. It also has no validated recovery function, so you cannot calculate how long to rest between dives.

ACCURATE BUT IMPRACTICAL

Arieli's index predicts pulmonary oxygen toxicity significantly better than UPTD — especially for short, high-pO₂ exposures typical in surface diving. It captures the non-linear relationship between pO₂ and time far more accurately.

The problem: Time is squared in the formula. For a multi-segment dive, calculating accumulated K requires complex square-root sums that are error-prone in the field.

Arieli K Formula

ESOT ✓

Equivalent Surface Oxygen Time. Mathematically ESOT = 60 × √K — same predictive accuracy as Arieli K, but expressing the result as minutes of equivalent oxygen exposure at the surface transforms a complex calculation into simple addition: calculate each dive segment's ESOT, then add them.

ESOT = 1 means one minute of breathing 100% O₂ at surface pressure. This intuitive reference point makes it easy to plan and communicate oxygen exposure. DMAC 35 Rev.1 (Feb 2025) formally recommends replacing UPTD with ESOT.

Before you can calculate ESOT, you need the partial pressure of oxygen (pO₂) at the depth you are diving. The calculator in Section 03 does this for you. The formula is:

Examples: Air (21% O₂) at 27 msw gives pO₂ = 3.7 × 0.21 = 0.78 atm. Nitrox 40 at 24 msw gives pO₂ = 3.4 × 0.40 = 1.36 atm, which the guide rounds to 1.4 atm for table lookups.

Common confusion: FiO₂ vs pO₂

FiO₂ is the fraction (percentage) of oxygen in the gas mix — e.g. 0.40 for Nitrox 40. pO₂ is the partial pressure of that oxygen at depth, which increases with depth. ESOT is calculated using pO₂, not FiO₂. Always calculate pO₂ first using Section 03 before entering values into the ESOT calculators.

3. The ESOT Formulas in Detail (Section 04 of the Guide)

Section 04 of the guide is the mathematical core. It presents all six DMAC 35 equations with plain-language explanations. This section of the companion document explains each one in full.

3.1 Equation 3 — Basic ESOT

This is the core formula. Multiply exposure time by pO₂ raised to the power 2.285. The exponent 2.285 captures the non-linear relationship between oxygen pressure and toxicity — doubling pO₂ more than doubles the toxic load. The toxicity weighting factor kp = pO₂^2.285 is pre-calculated in the lookup tables (Tables 3 and 4 in DMAC 35, and the Section 09 table in the guide).

3.2 Equation 4 — Accumulated ESOT for Multi-Segment Dives

For dives with more than one pO₂ (e.g. a bottom phase at 1.4 atm followed by an in-water decompression stop at 0.5 atm, then a chamber O₂ phase at 2.2 atm), calculate ESOT separately for each segment and add them together. Air breaks can be ignored — air breathing at normal diving depths contributes negligible ESOT.

The Dive Planner in Section 07 of the guide handles this automatically for up to four segments.

3.3 Equation 5 — Recovery (Residual ESOT)

This equation answers: "How much ESOT remains from my first dive after X hours of surface rest?" The residual ESOT is added to the ESOT of the next dive to give the total cumulative load.

Critical points about Equation 5:

  • The recovery rate depends on how long the preceding exposure lasted (texp), NOT on what pO₂ was used. Two divers with identical ESOT but different dive durations recover at different speeds — the one with the longer dive recovers more slowly.
  • This equation applies within a 24-hour period. Any surface interval of 12 hours or more resets the ESOT clock to zero for planning purposes.
  • The 2025 revision changed the recovery variable from pO₂ (used in the original 2023 Risberg paper) to texp (exposure duration). Always use the 2025 form shown above.
  • This equation should not be applied across more than two consecutive exposures on the same day. For three or more same-day dives, texp accumulates — see the explanatory text in Section 08 of the guide.

Why does the recovery rate depend on dive duration, not pO₂?

The lung tissue damage caused by a given ESOT is not simply reversed at a fixed rate. Longer exposures cause a pattern of injury that heals more slowly, even if the total ESOT value is identical to a shorter, higher-pO₂ exposure. Equation 5 captures this empirically validated behaviour. It is one of the key scientific advantages of the ESOT model over UPTD.

3.4 Equation 6 — Predicted VC Reduction

This equation predicts the expected reduction in Vital Capacity (VC) as a percentage. VC is the standard clinical measure of pulmonary oxygen toxicity — a measurable reduction indicates real lung function impairment.

Two important caveats: (1) This is a median population prediction. Individual variation is very large — some divers show significantly more VC reduction than the formula predicts, others significantly less. (2) At ESOT = 660 (the single-day limit), the formula predicts roughly 0.88% VC reduction — modest, but the limit is set conservatively to protect the more sensitive portion of the diving population.

3.5 UPTD Conversion Formula

This formula allows historical UPTD-based records to be compared against ESOT limits. Note that ESOT and UPTD are only identical at pO₂ = 1.0 atm (100% O₂ at the surface). They diverge at other pressures, with UPTD underestimating toxicity at low pO₂ and overestimating at high pO₂ relative to ESOT.

Understanding ESOT — The Science

Five equations govern how ESOT is calculated, accumulated, and how it recovers during surface intervals. Each is explained in plain English before the mathematics.

Basic ESOT (DMAC 35 Eq. 3)

Plain English: Multiply your dive time by a factor (kp) that grows rapidly with oxygen pressure. At pO₂ = 1.0 atm, kp = exactly 1.0. At pO₂ = 1.6 atm, kp = 2.93 — nearly three times worse than breathing surface oxygen for the same duration.

DMAC 35 Equation 3
ESOT = t × pO₂2.285
t = exposure time in minutes · pO₂ = partial pressure of O₂ in atm (from Section 03)
1

Calculate pO₂ using the Section 03 calculator

You need depth and gas mix fraction. The result (in atm) is what you raise to the power 2.285.

2

Raise pO₂ to the power 2.285 to get kp

This is the toxicity weighting factor. It is pre-calculated in the Section 09 lookup table for common pO₂ values. Use a calculator for other values.

3

Multiply kp by the time (minutes) to get ESOT

ESOT = 1 means one minute breathing 100% O₂ at surface pressure. Everything scales from this reference point.

💡
The Reference Point

At pO₂ = 1.0 atm: kp = 1.02.285 = 1.0. So 1 minute of breathing 100% O₂ at the surface = 1 ESOT exactly. Every other combination scales up from there. → ESOT Calculator (Section 06)

Accumulated ESOT — Multi-Segment Dives (DMAC 35 Eq. 4)

Plain English: When a dive has several phases at different depths or gas mixes, calculate ESOT for each segment separately — then simply add them together. This is ESOT's key operational advantage over Arieli K.

DMAC 35 Equation 4
ESOTacc = ESOT₁ + ESOT₂ + … + ESOTn
📐
Why simple addition works

With Arieli K, time is squared — so you cannot simply add segments together. The mathematical transformation that converts K to ESOT (ESOT = 60 × √K) linearises the accumulation, making addition valid. This is the entire reason ESOT was developed.

📋
Practical note: Air breaks

When calculating ESOT for a dive that includes air breaks, the air break period may be ignored — air breathing at normal diving depths contributes negligible ESOT (pO₂ ≈ 0.21–0.5 atm, kp ≈ 0.02–0.21). This simplification is explicitly permitted by DMAC 35. Operational standard for air break frequency: 5 minutes of compressed air breathing for every 20–30 minutes of O₂ breathing. DCD procedures mandate 5 min per 20 min; USN requires 5 min per 30 min. These breaks slow the development of both pulmonary and CNS oxygen toxicity and should always be included in dive planning even though they do not contribute to ESOT accumulation.

ESOT Recovery (DMAC 35 Eq. 5 — Revised 2025)

Plain English: After surfacing, accumulated lung exposure gradually heals. How fast you recover depends on how long the previous exposure lasted — not the depth or gas mix. Longer exposures produce a slower recovery rate. Use this equation to calculate residual ESOT before a repeat dive.

DMAC 35 Equation 5 (Revised 2025)
ESOTrec = ESOTi × e–r · stexp · t
r = 0.439 · s = 0.906 · texp = exposure duration (hours) · t = recovery time (hours)
⚠️
Important: 2025 formula change

The original 2023 Risberg paper used pO₂ as the recovery variable. DMAC 35 Rev.1 (2025) adopted a revised, better-validated model where recovery rate depends on exposure duration (texp). Always use this version for operational planning. → Recovery Calculator (Section 08)

Predicting Lung Impact — VC Reduction (DMAC 35 Eq. 6)

Plain English: This equation converts accumulated ESOT into a predicted percentage reduction in Vital Capacity (VC) — how much smaller your maximum lung volume becomes. The relationship is parabolic: doubling ESOT quadruples the predicted impact.

DMAC 35 Equation 6
ΔVC = 0.0082 × (ESOT / 60)²
📊
Individual variation is large

These are median population values — some divers will show significantly more VC reduction, others less. → VC Reduction Calculator (Section 06). The limits in DMAC 35 are set conservatively to protect the more sensitive majority of divers.

Converting Historical UPTD Records to ESOT

Plain English: If you have historical dive records in UPTD and need to compare them against ESOT limits, use this conversion. Note that at pO₂ = 1.0 atm (100% O₂ at surface), ESOT = UPTD exactly. They diverge at other pressures because the two indices weight pO₂ differently.

Conversion Formula
ESOT = UPTD × pO₂2.285 × (0.5 / (pO₂ – 0.5))5/6

For multi-segment dives, each segment must be converted separately using its own pO₂.

4.3 When to Call the Diving Medical Advisor

DMAC 35 explicitly requires a Diving Medical Advisor (DMA) in the following situations:

  • Any planned diving beyond 7 consecutive days of hyperoxic exposure
  • A diver reports respiratory symptoms at any exposure level
  • A risk assessment is needed to relax any limit (e.g. shorter than 12h overnight interval)
  • Planned multi-week SurDO₂ or TUP operations
  • Divers with pre-existing respiratory conditions

DMAC 35 Rev.1 — February 2025

The Diving Medical Advisory Committee formally recommends ESOT as the exposure index for pulmonary oxygen toxicity in surface-oriented diving. This section covers what DMAC 35 is, who it applies to, and what is explicitly excluded.

🏛️
What is DMAC 35?

DMAC 35 is a guidance note published by the Diving Medical Advisory Committee (dmac-diving.org). Revision 1 (February 2025) supersedes the June 2023 original. It establishes ESOT as the official replacement for UPTD with updated, scientifically validated exposure limits. Next scheduled revision: before February 2030.

Who Must Calculate ESOT?

✓
Must calculate ESOT

Any surface-oriented diver breathing gas with FiO₂ > 21%. This includes nitrox bottom mixes, oxygen-enriched mixes, and pure O₂ during SurDO₂ or TUP decompression.

🌊
No calculation needed

Divers breathing compressed air only. Air dives with in-water decompression adhering to HSE bottom-time restrictions are not expected to cause relevant POT — and can be done on designated recovery days.

Scope Exclusions

⚠️
DMAC 35 does not apply to:

• Saturation diving (different exposure pattern and physiology)
• Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO) therapy (resting patients, intermittent breathing)
• Military rebreather diving with high fixed pO₂
• CNS oxygen toxicity — managed separately via CNS clock limits

🔔
TUP (Transfer Under Pressure) diving

Transfer Under Pressure diving — where the diver surfaces in a closed bell and decompresses in a deck chamber — is a growing commercial method that sits between surface-oriented and saturation diving. ESOT applies directly to the oxygen breathing phases of TUP decompression. The TUP decompression tables developed by Sterk (DCD TUP / AoxTUP2B) and used commercially in the North Sea are designed with compatible oxygen exposure limits.

5.3 Section 08 — Recovery Calculator

This is the implementation of DMAC 35 Equation 5. It answers: after my first dive, how much ESOT remains going into my second dive?

Inputs:

  • Initial ESOT (the total ESOT from the first dive)
  • Exposure duration (texp) of the first dive in hours
  • Recovery time (surface interval) in hours

Outputs:

  • Residual ESOT: the amount remaining after the surface interval
  • Recovery fraction: what percentage of the original ESOT persists
  • A chart showing recovery curves for six different exposure durations (0.5h to 6h), based on DMAC Figure 1

The residual ESOT from this calculator is added to the ESOT of the second dive to give the total accumulated exposure for the day.

12-hour rule

If the surface interval is 12 hours or more, the residual ESOT is treated as zero for planning purposes. You do not need to use the recovery calculator in this case — simply start fresh with the new dive's ESOT. This is why DMAC 35 mandates a 12h overnight normoxic interval.

7. Recovery Curves (Section 08 — Figure 1)

The recovery chart in Section 08 is an interactive reproduction of DMAC 35 Figure 1. It shows six curves, one for each preceding exposure duration from 0.5 hours (fastest recovery) to 6 hours (slowest recovery). The Y-axis is logarithmic and shows residual ESOT as a fraction of the original (1.0 = 100% remains; 0.1 = 10% remains).

7.1 How to Read the Chart
  • Identify the preceding exposure duration (texp in hours)
  • Select the curve equal to or longer than your texp (conservative approach, as recommended by DMAC 35)
  • Find your surface interval on the X-axis
  • Read the relative dose on the Y-axis
  • Multiply the original ESOT by that fraction to get residual ESOT

Example from DMAC 35 Appendix I: First dive of 160 min (approx. 2.67h) at pO₂ = 1.4 atm, ESOT = 346. Surface interval = 5 hours. Using the 3-hour curve (conservative), residual ≈20%. Residual ESOT = 346 × 0.20 = 69. Second dive ESOT = 130. Total = 69 + 130 = 199.

7.2 Important Limitations
  • Figure 1 should not be used for more than two successive exposures on the same day. For three or more exposures, texp must accumulate (see Equation 5 explanatory text in DMAC 35 Appendix II).
  • The chart is a graphical approximation. The Section 08 calculator uses Equation 5 directly and is more precise.
  • The slider in the interactive chart allows you to drag the surface interval and watch residual ESOT change in real time.

Slide to 12h and every curve approaches or falls below 1% residual — at that point the difference between exposure durations is operationally negligible and the clock is treated as zeroed. 6.1 How the Table Works

The table is indexed by pO₂ (rows) and exposure time in minutes (columns). The value at each intersection is the pre-calculated ESOT for that combination. The table covers pO₂ from 0.5 to 2.5 atm and times from 15 to 240 minutes.

Mandatory 12h overnight normoxic interval

Every 24h period must include at least 12 continuous hours breathing normal air. This applies to all multi-day hyperoxic diving operations without exception.

☀️
Air breaks delay POT development

Intermittent breathing of compressed air during hyperoxic exposure slows the development of pulmonary oxygen toxicity. This can allow relaxation of certain limits with proper medical oversight.

📅
Air dives permitted on recovery days

Air-only in-water dives adhering to HSE bottom-time restrictions are permitted on designated "off" days. This flexibility allows continued operations during recovery periods.

📅
Two recovery days after any multi-day series

Following any hyperoxic diving series (5 or 7 day), the diver should have two days off (or air diving only) before resuming nitrox or SurDO₂ diving.

👤
Individual variation is large

No limit protects everyone equally. Some divers develop symptoms at lower doses. Report any respiratory symptoms immediately — even if calculated ESOT is within limits.

Any diving beyond 7 consecutive days, symptoms at low exposure, planned SurDO₂ multi-week operations, or any diver with pre-existing respiratory conditions.

DMAC 35 itself acknowledges that there is large individual variation in susceptibility to POT and that the models are not infallible. 10. Frequently Asked Questions

Q: The UPTD panel shows a different number to ESOT for the same dive. Which do I use?

Always use ESOT for operational planning under DMAC 35. UPTD is shown for historical comparison only and cannot be checked against DMAC 35 limits. The two indices only agree at pO₂ = 1.0 atm.

Q: My daily ESOT is 420 for 10 consecutive days. Is that within limits?

No. 420 ESOT/day is within the 7-day tier (≤450), but 10 consecutive days exceeds the maximum of 7 days for that tier. After 7 days at this level, 2 rest days are required. Beyond 7 consecutive hyperoxic days in total, a Diving Medical Advisor must be consulted regardless of daily ESOT.

Q: I have historical dive records in UPTD. Do I need to convert them?

Only if you want to compare them against ESOT-based limits. Use the UPTD → ESOT converter in Section 06 of the guide (under the Legacy Records collapsible). Note that the conversion requires knowing the pO₂ of the original exposure, not just the UPTD value.

Q: Can I use the guide on a tablet or phone?

Yes. The HTML is fully responsive and designed to work on any screen size. Open it in the device's web browser just as you would on a desktop.

Q: My surface interval is 8 hours. Do I need to calculate residual ESOT?

Yes — the 8-hour surface interval is less than the 12-hour threshold. Use Section 08 of the guide or Equation 5 directly. Enter the initial ESOT, the exposure duration (texp) of the first dive, and 8 hours as the recovery time. Add the resulting residual ESOT to the ESOT of the second dive.

Q: The guide mentions that air breaks slow POT. Does this mean I don't need to track ESOT during air breaks?

Correct — ESOT does not accumulate during air breaks because the pO₂ is negligible (approximately 0.21 atm at surface). Air break periods do not need to be included in ESOT calculations. The operational standard for air break frequency is 5 minutes of air per 20–30 minutes of O₂ breathing, depending on the procedure in use (DCD: 5/20; USN: 5/30).

Q: DMAC 35 says 660 ESOT is the single-day maximum. Does that mean exactly 660 is acceptable?

DMAC 35 states the dive should be planned to keep ESOT lower than 660. Values right at the limit should be reviewed carefully. The 48-hour recovery requirement after a 501–660 ESOT day is mandatory. For planned multi-day series, it is strongly advisable to keep daily ESOT below 500 or 450 to maintain multi-day operational flexibility.

Q: The formula for recovery uses the exponent s^texp. What if texp is longer than 6 hours?

Equation 5 has no mathematical upper limit on texp. However, DMAC 35 Figure 1 (which the guide reproduces) only shows curves up to 6 hours, and DMAC 35 Appendix I notes the figure should not be used for more than two consecutive exposures on the same day. For texp exceeding 6 hours or complex same-day multi-dive scenarios, the calculator in Section 08 applies Equation 5 directly. Very long texp values will produce a slow recovery rate — meaning more residual ESOT carries over. When in doubt, apply a conservative (longer) texp estimate.

A Counter-Perspective: Is OTU Still Valid?

Now that you fully understand ESOT and DMAC 35, you are in a position to evaluate this debate. Prof. Dr. W. Sterk (The Netherlands) is not only an independent diving medicine researcher — he is the developer of the proprietary AoxTUP2B (DCD TUP) decompression tables, used commercially by N-Sea, described as the single largest operator of North Sea offshore TUP diving in recent years. His arguments against replacing OTU with ESOT — grounded in decades of operational experience and real-world table development — deserve careful consideration alongside the formal standard.

📌
Context for the Reader

This section presents Sterk's arguments as fairly as possible. DMAC 35 is the current formal regulatory standard. Sterk's critique represents a legitimate and experienced counter-view. Both perspectives are presented here because operational divers and supervisors benefit from understanding the ongoing scientific debate.

Sterk's Core Arguments

ARGUMENT 1

Minimal Practical Difference

When ESOT and OTU values are calculated side by side within real decompression tables, the numerical differences are small. Operational decision thresholds remain essentially unchanged. The theoretical refinement may not translate to meaningful field benefit.

ARGUMENT 2

40 Years of Validated OTU Evidence

OTU-based limits have approximately four decades of operational application, including the extensive Oosterschelde storm surge barrier project (1984–1988). No evidence of unacceptable oxygen-related morbidity has emerged when limits are properly followed. ESOT has not yet matched this empirical record.

ARGUMENT 3

Systemic Symptoms Not Modelled

Oosterschelde divers showed oxygen-related symptoms beyond lung function changes: extreme fatigue, musculoskeletal pain, paresthesia, dizziness, nausea, headache — some resembling DCS. Neither ESOT nor UPTD models these systemic effects, leaving an unresolved gap in any purely pulmonary index.

ARGUMENT 4

Recovery Modelling Questioned

ESOT's modelled recovery suggests near-elimination after ~12 hours. Yet DMAC 35 still mandates non-diving periods after prolonged exposure — implicitly recognising that the mathematical recovery model may not fully reflect physiological reality. Cumulative systemic stress may persist beyond what the formula predicts.

ARGUMENT 5

Operational Simplicity Matters

Increased computational complexity without demonstrable safety benefit may introduce confusion in the field. The OTU system, widely understood and embedded in existing procedures, delivers operational clarity that ESOT has not yet fully replaced in practice.

ARGUMENT 6

Validation Gap

Long-term field validation of ESOT comparable to OTU-based systems is still lacking. ESOT is mathematically sound, but operational diving safety demands more than theory — it requires accumulated real-world evidence. Replacing established limits before that evidence exists carries its own risk.

The Oosterschelde Evidence Base

Oosterschelde Storm Surge Barrier Project, Netherlands (1984–1988)

One of the largest civil engineering dive operations in history — a key dataset for operational oxygen exposure research

This project generated extensive real-world data on repetitive hyperoxic exposure in working divers. The observation of systemic symptoms — some mimicking DCS — highlighted that cumulative oxygen stress may have physiological dimensions beyond what any single pulmonary index captures. In the field, differential diagnosis between oxygen toxicity and DCS was difficult without laboratory testing.

Where Both Sides Agree

✓
Points of convergence

Both ESOT/DMAC 35 and Sterk's OTU framework converge on approximately 450 units/day as a safe limit for multi-day operations. Both systems require overnight recovery and rest days after extended series. Both acknowledge individual variation. The debate is about the underlying model — not about the fundamental need for exposure limits.

📋
Bottom Line

DMAC 35 Rev.1 (2025) is the current formal regulatory standard and should be followed operationally. Sterk's concerns about systemic effects and overconfidence in recovery models deserve ongoing scientific attention. Both perspectives sharpen your understanding of why monitoring oxygen exposure is not simply a mathematical exercise — it involves real physiological complexity.

What This Means in Practice

✓
Follow DMAC 35 — it is the law

ESOT and the DMAC 35 limits are the required regulatory framework. Compliance is not optional. Use this guide's calculators and stay within the published limits.

👂
Take symptoms seriously at any ESOT level

Sterk's systemic symptom data means you should not dismiss fatigue, joint pain, or neurological symptoms simply because your calculated ESOT is within limits. Report everything to your Diving Medical Advisor.

📉
Treat limits as ceilings, not targets

Sterk's validation concern is best addressed by accumulating less exposure over time, not more. Experienced supervisors plan dives well below the maximum — the limits exist for emergencies, not routine daily targets.

🔬
Recovery models are estimates — behave accordingly

The Eq. 5 recovery curve is well-validated but not infallible. Mandatory rest days exist precisely because the mathematical model cannot capture all individual variation. Do not use the calculator to justify cutting rest short.
