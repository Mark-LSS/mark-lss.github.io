Extended Air Diving Umbilical Procedure
Training Package

This package turns the procedure into an interactive learning tool with visual explanations, step-by-step guided learning, calculators, checklists, scenario training and built-in tests. It is designed to make the procedure easier to learn, easier to revise, and easier to use during familiarisation.

Source basis: Extended Air Diving Umbilical Procedure, including requirements for hazard clearance, standby allowance, Golden Gate / in-water tending, marking system, shallow-water limits, pre-task actions, deployment, recovery, emergency recovery, and calculation logic.

Why this matters

Training focus

The procedure exists to let divers work with extended umbilicals while remaining clear of identified hazards such as thrusters, propellers, intakes and other vessel or subsea threats.

Core rule
+2 m

Standby diver / basketman reach is two metres longer than the working diver, while still staying outside the reduced standby hazard clearance.

Bailout planning
62.5 L/min

Emergency breathing rate used for endurance planning, with one minute of bailout required for every 10 m of umbilical deployed.

Learning path

1
Understand the purpose.
Why extended umbilical operations need planning, restrictions, verified dimensions and vessel-specific diagrams.
2
Learn the hazard model.
Thrusters, intakes, discharges, mooring lines, structures, currents, visibility and position reference limitations.
3
Learn how the lengths are set.
Working diver clearance, standby diver clearance, Golden Gate / in-water tending constraints and pre-task fitting positions.
4
Practice procedures.
Pre-task, deployment, return, standby deployment and standby recovery.
5
Pass the assessment.
Quick quiz, longer knowledge test and scenario test with scored results.

What the source procedure requires

Risk assessment content

Depth, currents, tidal stream, vessel velocity and heading-change limitations, hull features, and review of current umbilical management procedures.

Hazard control principle

All deployed umbilicals must be physically prevented from contacting identified hazards. Guards on thrusters and propellers are not accepted as a control measure on their own.

Verified dimensions

Dimensions and drawings must be verified by competent persons. 3D calculations are required. The diver helmet is treated as the closest point to the hazard when measuring stopper positions.

Operational pathways

The package covers standard basket deployment logic, Golden Gate / passive tending, standby swim line use, shallow-water issues, and emergency recovery sequence.

Rebuilt diagrams

Diagram lab: rebuilt visuals with highlights

These are rebuilt training diagrams based on the procedure logic. Each one has click targets, clean labels, and a Show explanation button so trainees can move between picture-first learning and the written rule.

1. Basket to Golden Gate to diver route

Click the yellow numbers
Select a hotspot or open the explanation.
1 Basket: controlled starting point
2 Golden Gate: control / pass-through point
3 Diver: actual working reach
4 Hazard: no-go zone remains protected
Explanation:
The rebuilt route diagram turns the written procedure into one picture. The diver does not get a free, unlimited excursion from the basket. The route is deliberately controlled through a fixed point, then the last leg is limited so the diver and umbilical cannot reach the nearest hazard. That is the real training message.

2. Hazard clearance geometry

Built for the calculators
Select a hotspot or open the explanation.
1 Distance build-up: B1, A'1 and D
2 Radius: hazard + margin
3 Clearance: subtract 5 m or 3 m
Purpose: converts geometry into safe reach
Explanation:
This picture matches the logic behind the safe-length calculator. First establish the 3D distance from deployment point to hazard centre. Then subtract the hazard radius and the required diver clearance. The result is not “how far the diver can probably get.” It is the maximum safe length.

3. Active versus passive tending

Decision-quality visual
Select a hotspot or open the explanation.
1 Active: direct umbilical control at the point
2 Passive: fixed pass-through only
3 Risk: slower or more difficult emergency recovery
Use case: passive is not the default
Explanation:
This is one of the most important judgement diagrams in the package. Active tending is the stronger control because someone is physically managing the umbilical. Passive tending may still be workable, but it must be justified, limited, and treated cautiously because recovery can become slower and more dangerous.

4. Emergency recovery route

Response picture
Select a hotspot or open the explanation.
1 Standby diver: goes to the casualty
2 Route: bring both divers back through the controlled path
3 Basket: recovery handover point
Rule: recovery route must still stay clear of hazards
Explanation:
Emergency recovery is not a random tow back to the surface. The diagram shows the procedure logic: standby reaches the injured diver, both are brought back through the controlled route, and the basket becomes the recovery handover point. The protected path still matters during an emergency.

Calculator 1: bailout endurance requirement

Based on the stated planning rule: enough bailout for 1 minute for every 10 m of umbilical deployed, using 62.5 L/min emergency consumption.

Calculator 2: safe umbilical length to hazard

Uses the procedure formula for basket-to-hazard geometry. Working diver uses R + 5. Standby diver uses R + 3.

Calculator 3: passive / Golden Gate C max

For passive or active in-water tending. The source rule states: C max = D - 5 or A - 5, whichever is shorter, and B must stay less than C.

Calculator 4: working diver length from standby basis

Used when the standby diver's opposite-hazard calculation becomes limiting. Source relation: Lw = Ls - 2 + LL.

Quick hazard cues

!
Never rely on thruster guards.
They do not protect divers or umbilicals adequately.
3D
3D calculations are required.
Distances and drawings must be verified and signed off by competent persons.
5
Working diver clearance is 5 m.
Measured from hazard radius boundary, including safety margin.
3
Standby diver clearance is 3 m.
Standby still needs +2 m operational reach compared with working diver.
SW
Shallow water increases risk.
DP reference reliability, thruster effects, current, visibility and vessel motion all matter more.

Pre-task activities

1
Select the next shallowest dive depth from the diagrams to choose the safe diver and standby lengths.
2
Confirm measured horizontal distances from diver and standby LARS to nearest thrusters agree with the diagrams and dive plan.
3
Confirm measured umbilical lengths, swim line lengths, and correct D-ring / carabiner / stopper positions.
4
Fit 3 m carabiners back from each diver's harness, fit working carabiners at distance C, and secure stoppers at B+C or B+C+2.
5
Fit and activate strobe and mini beacon. Attach aft and forward swim lines to the Golden Gate. Fit standby swim line.

Deployment and return

Deployment to worksite

Diver 1 confirms swim lines are horizontal and under light tension, clips to aft swim line, moves to the Golden Gate, pulls free umbilical through until carabiner reaches the gate, then proceeds to the worksite. Diver 2 repeats on the forward swim line.

Return to basket

Tender comes up on slack. Diver returns through the Golden Gate, reconnects 3 m carabiner to the correct swim line, follows the swim line back to the basket, disconnects the yellow carabiner at the basket, then tenders recover slack.

Standby diver deployment

Standby basket lands on the clump. Standby diver swims to Diver 1 basket, secures 3 m carabiner on forward swim line, pulls through until yellow carabiner is in position, then proceeds through the Golden Gate following Diver 1 umbilical.

Standby diver recovery

Standby diver recovers injured diver back through the Golden Gate, both 3 m carabiners are connected to the aft swim line, both yellow carabiners are disconnected at the basket, slack is recovered, and standby remains in Diver 1 basket for surface recovery.
